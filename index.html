    About

    Yuan Yang, a doctoral student in the public health program at Stony Brook University, is conducting her research under the guidance of Dr. Sean Clouston in the Social Brain Aging Laboratory. Her study focuses on examining the impact of various rescue activities performed by World Trade Center responders on their mental health as they age.

    Research

    WTC Rescue Activities and Cognition Health

    The tragic incidents of September 11 at the World Trade Center led to significant environmental dangers at the location. This included the dispersal of hazardous fine particulate matter (PM2.5), known to be a risk factor for dementia, which affected thousands of responders. Yuan had formulated a new set of rescue activity variables based on the potential Particulate Matter 2.5 (PM 2.5) derived from both structured and unstructured data sources. Her objective is to establish the correlation between activities and cognitive function both cross-sectionally and over time, and to further explore how different types of activities impact biomarkers derived from blood plasma and brain MRI scans. The most recent finging can be found on this published abstract.

    Interaction Effect of Education and Neuropathology on Cognitive Outcomes

    Yuan has led a meta-analysis to investigate the education moderate role between brain pathology and cognition. Education, as one of the modifiable risk factors, it is important to understand how education interacts with neuropathology on cognition. However, to date, there is no such systematic review for education as a moderator in the associations between neuropathology and cognition. This meta analysis is preregistered in the PROSPERO and is under development.

    Cognition Trajectory in Psychosis

    Yuan also worked together with Dr. Katherine Jonas on the Suffolk County Mental Health Project, a longitudinal cohort study focusing on first-time admissions of individuals with psychotic disorders. Her objective is to identify predictors and outcomes associated with the course of cognition in psychotic disorders.
